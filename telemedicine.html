I led generative research to turn an ambiguous remote physical-exam concept into a clinically grounded MVP direction. The work clarified what a first version needed to capture, what could safely wait, and which workflow constraints the product could not violate.

Overview

The challenge

The product concept was a remote physical-exam system: an on-site clinician performs a neck and thyroid exam while wearing sensorized gloves, and a remote specialist receives corresponding tactile feedback while watching the live video feed.

The team did not yet know what the product needed to capture for the experience to be clinically meaningful. Before engineering invested in sensors or design defined the interface, we needed to understand the real clinical workflow and where simplification would create risk.

What I needed to clarify

  • Identify the core palpation motions needed for a first version of the system.
  • Understand which parts of clinician technique were flexible, adaptive, and clinically important.
  • Translate clinical behavior into MVP requirements that engineering could build from.
  • Resolve scope tradeoffs between clinical fidelity and technical feasibility.

Approach

I mapped the clinical workflow before the team built

Because nothing had been built yet, I focused on the clinical behaviors v1 would need to support. I started by reviewing medical texts, but quickly found that written descriptions missed the nuance of hand movement, pressure, and sequence. I pivoted to structured video task analysis and expert validation, translating observable clinical technique into MVP requirements, build priorities, and product tradeoffs.

Product question 01

What should v1 support first?

Which palpation motions were consistent enough across neck and thyroid exams to justify prioritizing in the MVP?

Product question 02

What should not be oversimplified?

Which motions, pressure changes, and repeat passes were essential to clinical reasoning, even if they added product complexity?

Product question 03

Where would product constraints create risk?

Which product decisions — such as rigid sequences or capped pressure — would disrupt how clinicians actually work?

Research findings

What the research revealed

The quantitative coding clarified motion priority, but the expert interview revealed the higher-stakes interaction constraint: the system could simplify motion scope, but it could not simplify clinician judgment into a rigid sequence.

Finding 01

Two motion types covered the majority of observed behavior

Circular motion and press/release appeared most consistently across exams and represented the strongest candidates for v1 sensing.

The research gave the team a defensible way to focus the MVP without guessing or overbuilding.

Recommendation: Prioritize circular and press/release in v1; defer lower-frequency motions into a future extension model.
Finding 02

Clinical value depended on adaptive pressure, not just motion capture

The specialist emphasized that clinicians vary pressure across multiple passes based on what they feel, especially when distinguishing mobile versus fixed tissue.

This meant pressure could not be treated as a fixed setting or a simple linear input.

Recommendation: Preserve variable pressure and unrestricted repeat passes; do not enforce a single exam flow.
Finding 03

Rigid workflows would create clinical risk

A guided step-by-step workflow may look simpler from a product perspective, but it would constrain the exact expert behavior the tool needed to support.

The product needed to support clinician autonomy rather than force standardization too early.

Recommendation: Use an open interaction model with flexible sequence, adaptive depth, and repeated passes.
Finding 04

Configurability was a product risk that needed testing

Design stakeholders wanted clinician-adjustable sensitivity controls, while engineering worried about complexity and instability.

The real question was whether clinicians would actively seek customization in v1.

Recommendation: Test intent with a fake-door prototype before committing engineering effort.
Expert validation themes

Physicians do not all follow the same palpation sequence.

What a clinician feels mid-exam changes what they do next.

Pressure variation and repeated passes are central to diagnostic reasoning.

De-risking scope

I proposed a fake-door test to de-risk an expensive feature

One unresolved product question was whether the MVP should include configurable sensitivity settings. Clinicians might need control over pressure thresholds, but engineering was concerned that settings would add instability and delay the build.

Rather than resolve the disagreement through opinion, I reframed it as a product experiment: would clinicians actually look for customization during setup?

Signal

Measure demand before implementation

Click-through would indicate whether clinicians noticed and actively sought configurability in context.

Follow-up

Separate need from curiosity

Short interviews with clickers would clarify whether the control reflected a real workflow need or simple exploration.

Decision

Define a defensible product path

If intent was low, defer settings. If intent was high and clinically grounded, prioritize settings with a narrower first implementation.

Why this mattered: The fake-door approach protected engineering time while still giving design and clinical stakeholders a fair way to validate a potential need.
