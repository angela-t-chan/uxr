Evaluating haptic feedback as a communication feature

I led end-to-end mixed-methods research to evaluate whether tactile feedback could become a meaningful communication feature in remote interactions. The work translated behavioral, survey, and physiological data into product recommendations for when haptic cues help, when they distract, and how future systems should give users control.

Context

Haptics can add a new communication channel — but only if it creates real user value.

The team was exploring whether haptic feedback could move beyond novelty and become a useful layer in remote communication. The product question was not simply whether people liked the device, but whether haptics changed how people communicated, when it added value, and what interaction rules should guide future product design.

To stress-test the concept, I evaluated the feature in a demanding remote communication scenario: partners discussing meaningful topics while physically apart. This made it possible to observe whether tactile cues were useful in moments where timing, intent, and emotional nuance mattered.

Product question 01

Does haptic feedback create value?

Does tactile feedback provide a meaningful benefit beyond novelty in remote communication?

Product question 02

When does it help or distract?

What moments, intentions, and conversational cues make haptic feedback feel useful, intrusive, or unnecessary?

Product question 03

What rules should guide the feature?

What interaction patterns should future systems support so haptic cues feel intentional, lightweight, and user-controlled?

For a product team, this work de-risked an emerging interaction concept: it clarified whether haptics should be treated as a meaningful product capability, what user behaviors it supported, and what design guardrails would be needed before building it into a broader communication experience.

Study design

A controlled evaluation of tactile feedback in remote communication

I recruited 20+ adult romantic couples who had been together for at least one year. Each partner joined from a separate location. Couples completed two discussion sessions — one with the tactile feedback device available and one without — enabling a direct comparison of the remote communication experience with and without haptic cues.

Shared context

Each partner watched the same 15-minute video clip featuring relationship and work-life themes to establish a common conversational starting point.

Discussion task

Couples discussed semi-structured prompts designed to create a realistic, high-intent communication scenario where nonverbal cues could matter.

Haptic condition

In one session, partners could exchange tactile cues through a wearable device while on the video call.

Reflection

Participants completed surveys and in-depth interviews about connection, communication quality, perceived value, and the role of tactile feedback.

Data collected

Audio and video recordings of all sessions
Touch exchange logs with timing and duration
Pre- and post-session surveys
Post-session interview transcripts
Physiological data via E4 Empatica wristbands
Conversation transcripts for sentiment analysis

Behavioral coding

I labeled touch intent by reviewing conversational context, facial expression, tone, timing, and touch duration and intensity.

Quantitative analysis

I analyzed survey responses and physiological signals to compare user response across conditions.

Sentiment analysis

I coded approximately 1,200 minutes of conversation transcript to connect touch behavior with conversational context and timing.

Findings

The feature's value depended on timing, intent, and conversational context

Participants did not use tactile feedback as a generic add-on. They used it in specific conversational moments — to acknowledge, reassure, soften tension, or signal presence. The data showed that value came less from frequency of use and more from whether the cue felt timely, intentional, and easy to control.

44%

Improvement in key user response

Reflected in lower electrodermal activity from E4 Empatica wristbands. The effect was most meaningful during high-intent moments, showing that tactile feedback changed the quality of the interaction rather than simply increasing feature use.

60+

Distinct touch intentions identified

Behavioral coding revealed over 60 touch intentions, grouped into affective uses (reassurance, care, affection, repair) and non-affective uses (acknowledgment, agreement, conversational pacing).

4

User value themes

Across interviews and surveys, participants consistently described tactile feedback as increasing presence, strengthening connection, adding nuance, and creating an additional interaction channel.

Q01 Does haptic feedback create value?
Yes. Physiological data showed a 44% improvement in a key user outcome in the haptic condition, reflected by lower electrodermal activity. Participants also reported higher perceived connection and communication quality in post-session surveys, connecting the finding to user experience and product value.
Q02 When does it help or distract?
When it was intentional and tied to the conversation. Tactile feedback worked best as a lightweight cue that reinforced, softened, or responded to what was already being said, rather than as a standalone signal. Behavioral coding identified over 60 distinct touch intentions, grouped into two categories:
Affective

Cues tied to high-intent moments — reassurance, care, affection, tension repair, shared presence.

Non-affective

Cues used functionally — acknowledgment, agreement, attention, conversational pacing.

Context mattered more than frequency. The value of a tactile exchange depended on when it occurred relative to the conversation — not simply how often the feature was used.
Q03 What rules should guide the feature?
Expressive control and context-awareness. Participants valued tactile feedback when it felt intentional, timely, and appropriate — not generic or mechanical. The design opportunity is not simply adding a haptic channel, but building systems where timing, intent, and nuance are part of the interaction model.
Design recommendations
Recommendation 01

Keep haptic cues lightweight and conversation-adjacent

Users should be able to send tactile cues without interrupting the video call or shifting attention away from the conversation.

Recommendation 02

Prioritize intentional cues over frequent cues

The value came from cues being timely and meaningful, not from high usage volume. Future systems should avoid encouraging constant haptic interaction.

Recommendation 03

Preserve expressive control

Because meaning depended on timing, intensity, and relationship context, users need control over when and how touch is sent rather than generic automated feedback.

Recommendation 04

Design for high-intent moments

Tactile feedback is most valuable when it supports reassurance, repair, affection, acknowledgment, or shared presence during meaningful moments in the conversation.

Impact

From research findings to product direction

The research clarified when tactile feedback could become a meaningful product capability rather than a technical demo. For product and design teams, the implication extended beyond haptics: nonverbal signals can create measurable value when they are lightweight, intentional, and user-controlled.

Product impact

Translated mixed-methods findings into interaction principles for haptic communication: keep cues lightweight, preserve user control, and design around timing and intent rather than novelty.

Research impact

Built a multimodal evaluation approach combining self-report, behavioral observation, conversational analysis, interaction logs, and physiological measurement to assess how users experienced a new interaction channel.

Strategic impact

Helped clarify whether haptics should be treated as a meaningful communication capability, not just a novel hardware interaction, by identifying where tactile feedback added value and where it could interrupt the experience.
