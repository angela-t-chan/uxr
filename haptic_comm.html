Remote touch during difficult conversations

An NSF-funded study examining whether tactile feedback could make video calls more emotionally supportive. I led the research end to end — study design, data collection, multimodal analysis, and product implications.

Context

Touch is a communication channel. Video calls don't have one.

Touch carries signals that words and facial expressions can't easily replicate — reassurance, care, tension repair, and shared presence. For people who rely on remote communication to maintain close relationships, that gap matters.

I focused on a deliberately demanding scenario: romantic partners navigating emotionally charged conversations while physically apart. This created a realistic test of whether mediated touch could provide meaningful support when emotional stakes are high.

Research question 01

Does remote touch change affect?

Does tactile feedback shift participants' emotional experience or physiological response during a difficult conversation?

Research question 02

How do people use touch in context?

What moments, intentions, and conversational cues lead people to send or respond to remote touch?

Research question 03

What should future systems support?

What do observed behaviors tell us about the design requirements for emotionally aware communication tools?

For a product team, this study answered a practical design question: whether haptics should be treated as a novelty feature, or as a meaningful communication channel with its own interaction rules. The goal was not only to measure emotional impact, but to understand what kinds of tactile interactions future communication systems should support.

Study design

A controlled study of touch in emotionally charged video calls

I recruited 20+ adult romantic couples who had been together for at least one year. Each partner joined from a separate location. Couples completed two discussion sessions — one with the remote touch device available and one without — enabling a direct comparison of the communication experience with and without tactile feedback.

Emotional setup

Each partner watched the same 15-minute video clip featuring relationship and work-life themes to establish shared conversational context.

Discussion task

Couples discussed semi-structured prompts designed to surface emotionally meaningful topics — for example, the difference between taking a break and breaking up.

Touch condition

In one session, partners could exchange tactile cues through a wearable remote touch device while on the video call.

Reflection

Participants completed PANAS surveys and in-depth interviews covering affect, connection, communication quality, and perceived role of touch.

Data collected

Audio and video recordings of all sessions
Touch exchange logs with timing and duration
Pre- and post-session PANAS affect surveys
Post-session interview transcripts
Physiological data via E4 Empatica wristbands
Conversation transcripts for sentiment analysis

Behavioral coding

I labeled touch intent by reviewing conversational context, facial expression, tone, timing, and touch duration and intensity.

Quantitative analysis

I analyzed affective survey responses and physiological signals to measure changes in emotional experience and physiological response across conditions.

Sentiment analysis

I coded approximately 1,200 minutes of conversation transcript to connect touch behavior with emotional tone in the dialogue.

Findings

The value of remote touch depended on timing, intent, and conversational context

Participants didn't treat touch as a supplementary feature. They used it in specific conversational moments — to reassure, soften tension, signal affection, or feel more present with their partner. The data showed consistent patterns across both subjective experience and physiological response.

44%

Improvement in emotional regulation

Reflected in lower electrodermal activity from E4 Empatica wristbands. The effect was most meaningful during moments of tension or emotional disclosure — showing that remote touch supported calmer, more connected conversations rather than simply increasing feature use.

60+

Distinct touch intentions identified

Behavioral coding revealed over 60 touch intentions, grouped into affective uses (reassurance, care, affection, repair) and non-affective uses (acknowledgment, agreement, conversational pacing).

4

Recurring experience themes

Across interviews and surveys, participants consistently described remote touch as enhancing presence, deepening connection, amplifying emotional meaning, and adding a new interaction channel to the conversation.

RQ01 Does remote touch change affect?
Yes. Physiological data showed a 44% improvement in emotional regulation in the touch condition, reflected by lower electrodermal activity. Participants also reported higher perceived connection and communication quality in post-session surveys, making the result easier to connect to user experience and product value.
RQ02 How do people use touch in context?
Intentionally and co-verbally. Touch was used as emotional punctuation — a way to reinforce, soften, or respond to what was already being said, rather than as a standalone signal. Behavioral coding identified over 60 distinct touch intentions, grouped into two categories:
Affective

Touch tied to emotional expression — reassurance, care, affection, tension repair, shared presence.

Non-affective

Touch used functionally — acknowledgment, agreement, attention, conversational pacing.

Context mattered more than frequency. The emotional value of a touch exchange depended on when it occurred relative to the conversation — not simply how often touch was used.
RQ03 What should future systems support?
Expressive control and context-awareness. Participants valued touch when it felt intentional, timely, and emotionally appropriate — not generic or mechanical. The design opportunity is not simply adding a haptic channel, but building systems where timing, intent, and nuance are part of the interaction model.
Design recommendations
Recommendation 01

Make touch lightweight and conversation-adjacent

Users should be able to send tactile cues without interrupting the video call or shifting attention away from the conversation.

Recommendation 02

Prioritize intentional touch over frequent touch

The value came from touch being timely and meaningful, not from high usage volume. Future systems should avoid encouraging constant haptic interaction.

Recommendation 03

Preserve expressive control

Because meaning depended on timing, intensity, and relationship context, users need control over when and how touch is sent rather than generic automated feedback.

Recommendation 04

Design for emotionally sensitive moments

Remote touch is most valuable when it supports reassurance, repair, affection, or shared presence during emotionally meaningful conversations.

Impact

From research findings to product direction

The study showed that mediated touch can function as a meaningful communication channel in emotionally sensitive remote interactions. For product and design teams, the implication extends beyond haptics: nonverbal signals have measurable emotional value, and communication tools can be designed to support them.

Design impact

The findings translated into concrete design principles for emotionally supportive communication tools: keep nonverbal signals lightweight, preserve user control, and design around timing and intent rather than novelty.

Research impact

I developed a multimodal evaluation approach that combined self-report, behavioral observation, conversational analysis, and physiological measurement. This made it possible to evaluate emotional experience with more depth than any single method would allow.

Scholarly impact

The work contributed to peer-reviewed research on remote touch communication and established a foundation for future studies on emotionally resonant digital communication systems.
